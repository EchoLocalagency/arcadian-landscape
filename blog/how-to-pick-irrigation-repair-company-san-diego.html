How to Pick an Irrigation Repair Company in San Diego

Published 2026-07-21 by Arcadian Landscape

Last updated: July 2026

How do you choose an irrigation repair company in San Diego?

Pick a contractor who tests static water pressure before quoting, holds a California C-27 or C-53 license, and repairs the cause rather than the symptom. Ask what PSI your system reads at the hose bib. If they cannot answer, they are guessing. Single-valve repairs typically run $185 to $450.

A homeowner in Carmel Valley called us about a wet spot in the lawn. Three companies had already been out. Two replaced spray heads. One replaced the same head twice.

Nobody put a gauge on the system.

The house was reading 92 PSI static. That yard did not have a head problem. It had a pressure problem that was splitting poly fittings underground, and every new head just moved the failure ten feet down the line.

The pressure test is not optional

Water pressure across San Diego County varies enormously by elevation and district. Coastal Encinitas, Cardiff, and Del Mar lots we work on usually gauge 55 to 70 PSI. Inland, up in Rancho Bernardo, 4S Ranch, and parts of Escondido, we regularly see 80 to 95 PSI at the hose bib.

Here is why that matters. Spray heads are designed to operate around 30 PSI at the nozzle. Drip tubing wants 25 to 30. Push 90 PSI into either one and you get misting, fogging, blown fittings, and a water bill nobody can explain.

The fix is a pressure regulator at the point of connection, or pressure-regulating filters at each drip valve. That is a real repair. Swapping heads is not.

We wrote more about the diagnostic sequence in our post on why San Diego irrigation repairs start with a pressure test.

What licensing should an irrigation contractor hold?

In California, irrigation work falls under a C-27 Landscape Contractor license or a C-53 for some specialty work. Arcadian Landscape holds California Contractor License #978145, a C-27, and we are bonded and insured.

Ask for the number. Then look it up. It takes ninety seconds on the state board site and it eliminates most of the field.

Those are ranges, not quotes. Access drives cost more than parts do. A valve box buried under eight years of mulch and a mature rosemary takes twice as long as one sitting in open turf.

Three questions worth asking on the phone

  • What is my static pressure, and how will you measure it?
  • Are you repairing the failure or the cause of the failure?
  • Will you leave me a zone map showing what each valve controls?

That last one matters more than people expect. Most of the yards we inherit have no documentation at all, and the previous owner took the knowledge with them. We label valve boxes and hand over a zone map on every irrigation job.

When repair stops making sense

There is a point where patching a thirty-year-old system costs more than replacing it. Galvanized mains, brittle sun-baked poly, four valves that all leak past, a controller nobody can program. We tell homeowners honestly when they have crossed that line.

On a renovation we are already building, new irrigation goes in while the trenches are open, which is dramatically cheaper than retrofitting later. That is covered on our irrigation and landscape renovation pages.

And if a repair we made in your neighborhood held up through the June gloom and the September heat, mentioning the specific work and the area in a Google review helps other homeowners more than a star rating does.
